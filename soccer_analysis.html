Soccer/Football Match outcome Prediction¶

Question: Which in-game performance metrics best predict the outcome of a soccer/football match?¶

Purpose: This project uses match and team statistics from the 2024-2025 season across 400+ leagues worldwide to build a machine learning classification model that predicts wether a team wins, draws, or loses based on thier in-game performance. The goal is to identify which tactical and statistical factors are the most effective at predicting match outcomes.¶

Dataset ESPN Soccer Data via Kaggle - includes 30,000+ Match fixtures and team-level statistics across the 24/25 season.¶

Approach:¶

  1. Data loading / merging
  2. Data cleaning
  3. Exploratory Data Analysis
  4. Feature Engineering
  5. Machine learning modeling and evaluation

Importing Data¶

Data loaded from the ESPN Soccer dataset. fixtures.csv: contains match results and team IDs teamStats.csv contains per-match performance statistics for each team teams.csv and leagues.csv provide reference information for filtering and joining later in the analysis.

Dataset Merge & Target Feature Engineering¶

I merged the team performance stats (teamStats.csv) with the match results (fixtures.csv) on eventId as thats the relational key. teamStats has two rows per match (one per team), so I created a 'result' column that label each teams row from thier perspective. This is the varaible our model will try to predict.

# Merge the datasets (Fixtures and Team Stats) to get match outcomes
df = pd.merge(fixtures[['eventId', 'homeTeamId', 'awayTeamId', 'homeTeamWinner', 'awayTeamWinner']], team_stats, on=['eventId'], how='left')
print(df.head())

# teamStats doesnt have who won the match, so label each row as win/draw/loss for the home team
def label_match_outcome(row):
    if row['teamId'] == row['homeTeamId']: # Checks if the team is home
        if row['homeTeamWinner'] == True:
               return 'Win' #If the home team won, label as 'Win'
        elif row['awayTeamWinner'] == True:
             return 'Loss' # If the away team won, label as 'Loss'
        else:
             return 'Draw'
    else:
        if row['awayTeamWinner'] == True: # Then the team is away
            return 'Win' # If the away team won, label as 'Win'
        elif row['homeTeamWinner'] == True:
            return 'Loss' # If the home team won, label as 'Loss'
        else:
             return 'Draw'

df['result'] = df.apply(label_match_outcome, axis=1)

Exploratory Data Visualizations¶

The distribution plot shows a balanced spread of wins and losses, with draws being less frequent. The scatter plot of possession vs shots on target reveals a clear trend that teams with more shots on target are more likely to win. However, possession alone does not show a clear relationship with match result, suggesting a team can have low possession and still get a result.

This is a well-known reality in the sport, as teams set out tactically to have more or less of the ball depending on their style of play. In professional analytics, this is captured through Expected Goals (xG) — a metric that represents how dangerous an opportunity was, accounting for shot location, body angle, and other factors. Since event-level data required for xG is beyond the scope of this dataset, proxy variables will be engineered to capture a team's attacking lethality.

Feature Engineering¶

Three metrics were engineered to better capture a team's performance and provide deeper analysis into its relationship with a match result.

Shot Efficiency: The ratio of shots on target to total shots. This captures a team's clinical quality - essentially, a higher value indicates a more clinincal team - going beyond simple volume that could be generated from shots. Teams with 0 total shots receive a value of 0, reflecting a true data point possible in a game.

Threat Per Possession: This metric captures a team's attacking output relative to the time on the ball. The metric, in a way, accounts for differing styles of play. A counter-attacking team can have low possession but high threat if they're clinical when the ball is in their favor.

Ball Recovery: Sum of effective tackles and interceptions. A metric that represents a team's defensive activity, with higher values suggesting a more aggressive pressing team or defensive style.

Note: These features serve as proxies for attacking threat and defensive activity given the dataset at hand.

# Feature Engineering

# Shot efficiency metric: how consistent do the shots hit the target
# A team with higher shot efficiency is more dangerous attacking-wise, which is a key factor in winning matches.
df_clean['shot_efficiency'] = df_clean['shotsOnTarget'] / df_clean['totalShots']

# Threat per possession: how much danger did you create relative to how much ball you had?
# Despite low possession, a team can still create a lot of danger if they are efficient with their attacking opportunities. This metric captures that dynamic.
df_clean['threat_per_possession'] = df_clean['shotsOnTarget'] / df_clean['possessionPct']

# Defensive metric: how well did you win the ball back?
# Combines tackles and interceptions into ball_recovery score
df_clean['ball_recovery'] = df_clean['effectiveTackles'] + df_clean['interceptions']

Engineered Feature Analysis¶

The boxplots show that shot efficiency has the clearest separation across results. Winning teams consistently put a higher share of their shots on target compared to draws and losses, making it the strongest of the three engineered features.

Threat per possession is tightly distributed across all three results with little variation. It captures an interesting tactical dynamic but may not be a strong standalone predictor. The outliers visible are legitimate data points reflecting extreme counter attacking performances, not errors, so they were kept. Ball recovery distributions are almost identical across wins, draws and losses, suggesting that how much a team wins the ball back matters less than what they do with it once they have it. A key limitation here is that the metric does not account for where on the pitch the recovery happened. Winning the ball back in the final third is a completely different tactical statement than winning it in your own half, something that would require positional event level data to properly capture.

Models¶

With the data being split into its appropriate training and testing sets, classification modeling was chosen as the most appropriate approach to identifying the relationship between performance metrics and results.

Models: Logistic Regression: Classification model that assumes a linear relationship between features and results. The model calculates the probability of each outcome and chooses the one with highest probability.

Random Forest: Handles nonlinear relationships and feature interactions which the Logistic Regression model cannot handle. This model builds hundreds of decision trees on random subsets of the data and features, where all trees come up with a outcome and based on majority vote the outcome is chosen accordingly.

Both models are evaluated using accuracy and classification report - the models were trained on a 80/20 split of the data.

Feature Importance¶

Ranking of which metrics the model relied on most when predicting the match result. A higher importance indicates that the model found that feature to be more useful across its decision trees.

The ranking of the metrics validates the engineering features, where two engineered proxy metrics ranked in the top 5 - threat per possession, which was the most important feature in the dataset, and shot efficiency ranked 5th. This confirms that capturing attacking lethality relative to possession and shot quality matters more to predicting outcomes than raw stats alone.

Possession percentage ranked second despite showing a weak Pearson correlation in EDA. This indicates that the model found it meaningful only when combined with other features rather than as a standalone predictor.

Fouls committed and won corners also ranked high, which logically makes sense. A team committing many fouls is often chasing the result or a team thats tactically disrupting the opponent's play, while winning corners reflects sustained dominant pressure on the opponent.

Confusion Matrix Analysis¶

All three models tell the same story. The Draw row is consistently the weakest across every model — the diagonal for Draw is nearly empty, meaning the models almost never confidently predicted a draw, instead mistaking them for either a Win or a Loss.

Win and Loss predictions are reasonably strong with dark diagonals across all three models, showing the models can distinguish between winning and losing performances with moderate ability, however, they struggle at predicting draws.

Model Evaluation¶

A third model, Gradient Boosting Classifier, was introduced — similar to Random Forest in using decision trees but adds the element of sequentially correcting each tree based on the errors of the last. It was added with the expectation that self correcting trees would improve prediction ability, however following its results all three models were nearly identical in performance.

Logistic Regression: 55% accuracy. Win F1 of 0.64, Loss F1 of 0.61, Draw F1 of 0.02. The model essentially gave up predicting draws.

Random Forest: 54% accuracy. Win F1 of 0.63, Loss F1 of 0.60, Draw F1 of 0.13. Slight improvement on draws while maintaining similar win and loss rates. Gradient Boosting: 56% accuracy. Win F1 of 0.65, Loss F1 of 0.62, Draw F1 of 0.02. Strongest overall but identical to Logistic Regression on draws.

Conclusion¶

Three models of increasing complexity all resulted around 55% accuracy. This is not a modeling failure, however, it conveys a ceiling in predicting match outcomes from team stats alone. Soccer/football is a highly variant sport where factors like in game events, weather, and more complex metrics such as xG, possession in the final third, and pressing intensity are more appropriate predictors. Given the scope of this dataset and the absence of event level positional data, no model complexity was found to break through this ceiling.
